The Entourage Effect: CBD, THC, and Terpenes Working Together

Published on June 15, 2025 by Kingkush Team

For years, the spotlight in cannabis research was primarily on THC (Tetrahydrocannabinol) and its psychoactive properties. More recently, CBD (Cannabidiol) gained immense popularity for its therapeutic benefits without the high. However, a growing body of scientific evidence supports a more holistic view of cannabis's effects, a phenomenon known as the "Entourage Effect."

What is the Entourage Effect?

The Entourage Effect is a theory proposed by Dr. Ethan Russo, a leading cannabis researcher, suggesting that cannabinoids (like THC and CBD), terpenes (aromatic compounds), and other compounds found in the cannabis plant work synergistically to enhance each other's therapeutic benefits. Instead of isolated compounds acting alone, they interact to create a combined effect that is greater than the sum of their individual parts.

The Key Players: Cannabinoids and Terpenes

While over 100 cannabinoids have been identified, THC and CBD are the most abundant and studied. But the entourage effect extends beyond them to include terpenes.

  • Cannabinoids (e.g., CBD, THC, CBG, CBN): These compounds interact directly or indirectly with the body's endocannabinoid system (ECS), which plays a role in regulating a wide range of physiological processes, including mood, pain sensation, appetite, and sleep.
  • Terpenes (e.g., Myrcene, Limonene, Pinene, Caryophyllene): These are aromatic compounds responsible for the diverse scents and flavors of cannabis (and many other plants). Beyond aroma, research suggests terpenes have their own therapeutic properties and can influence how cannabinoids interact with the body. For example, Myrcene is thought to have sedative effects, while Limonene is often associated with mood elevation.

How They Work Together

The beauty of the Entourage Effect lies in the intricate ways these compounds interact:

  • Modulating Effects: CBD, for instance, is believed to modulate some of THC's psychoactive effects, potentially reducing anxiety or paranoia that some users experience with high THC.
  • Enhanced Absorption: Some terpenes might enhance the absorption of cannabinoids into the bloodstream or across the blood-brain barrier.
  • Targeted Therapies: Specific combinations of cannabinoids and terpenes may be more effective for particular conditions. For example, a strain rich in Myrcene (sedative) and CBD might be more effective for sleep issues than pure CBD isolate.
  • Broadening Therapeutic Scope: The presence of a full spectrum of compounds means that the plant can address a wider array of symptoms and conditions than any single isolated compound could.

Full-Spectrum vs. Isolates

This understanding has led to a preference for "full-spectrum" cannabis products, which retain a wide range of cannabinoids, terpenes, and other plant compounds. In contrast, "isolates" contain only one cannabinoid (e.g., pure CBD). While isolates have their place, full-spectrum products are often favored for maximizing the entourage effect and potential therapeutic benefits.
